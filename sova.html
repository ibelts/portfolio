Sova

A platform for sharing
children’s paper books in Russian

Overview

Sova is a private library available for people located in the Research Triangle Park area, North Carolina. It provides a convenient way for parents to find, borrow, and lend books in Russian for their children. “Sova” means “an owl” in Russian.

Problem

Most Russian-speaking parents want their children to be able to speak their heritage language and regularly read books in Russian with them. Finding these books in the US is challenging. There are almost no local stores that offer literature in Russian so people usually have to order books online from Russian stores and pay for extremely expensive shipping. Children (especially younger) prefer to read paper books with large illustrations, so online books don’t work. The Russian community is in need of a reliable source of paper books for their children.

Solution

Sova is a free platform that allows people located in the RTP to exchange their books with others. Parents can create an account and list children’s paper books in Russian they would like to lend as well as search for books they would like to borrow. Using the platform they can keep track of books they are lending or borrowing and communicate with borrowers or book owners via a built-in Facebook messenger. Sova offers a powerful search as well as an opportunuty to browse the catalog of available books. The platform provides Russian parents with a reliable source of books in Russian for their children.

Discovery and Research

Competitive analysis

A majority of book sharing apps offer only e-books. Surprisingly, there are basically no solutions for creating a private library that would work with paper books. At the moment there’s one app that allows lending and borrowing paper books available across all cities in the US (Boocshare), one app is available in 8 US cities (Peerby, a platform for exchanging anything, including books). Other platforms for borrowing/lending books are not available in the US (such as ChapterOne).

Boocshare

  • + An ability to create groups and invite friends.

  • + A live support chat is available.

  • + The app is translated into three languages.

  • - There is no option to search for groups.

  • - There is no option to send a join request to the group.

  • - Users don’t load their avatars, almost all are placeholder images.

  • - There is no option to borrow if the user doesn’t want to lend their books.

Peerby

  • + Transparent and straightforward sharing/lending process.

  • + Modern-looking UI.

  • + The user can start searching for something right away without having to sign up.

  • + There is a sign up with a Facebook account option.

  • + The user can see statistics for every lender.

  • - Available only in 8 US cities (in BETA version).

  • - Website and messages that the user gets from the platform are in German.

ChapterOne

  • + A straightforward indication of all available services the app has to offer.

  • + Good grid structure.

  • + A full list of books on the homepage that is easy to browse.

  • + There is an option to view books by category.

  • - Available in India only.

  • - Confusing explanation of the books borrowing/lending process.

  • - No search field, a magnifying glass icon is hard to find.

User survey

I conducted a detailed user survey to find out goals, frustrations and motivations of potential app users. All participants of my survey represent the target audience of the library app - they are all parents of children who read in Russian.

There are three main sources of children’s books in Russian: friends and family members bring books from Russia when they visit (63.6%), parents order books online (59.1%), and borrow books from friends (27.3%).

The biggest frustrations of participants in their book search process are delivery cost (to ship books to the US from Russian stores), not being able to look at hard copies of a book they are buying online, buying books that they could’ve borrowed from friends, since they can’t ask all friends about all their books.

The survey revealed motivations of parents to read books in Russian with their children: “Preserving the Russian language and culture in our family,” “It is a bonding time,” “It’s a tradition in our family.”

Visual design

Logo

The platform is dedicated to childrens’ literature, however the target audience, who will be using the app are parents of Russian-speaking children. I wanted to combine youthfulness with robust usability. I used Adobe Illustrator to integrate an owl (“sova” means “an owl” in Russian) into the name of the app.

Typography

Libre Baskerville is a serif typeface based on the Baskerville font family that was originally designed by the American Type Founders (ATF) in 1841. Like the original Baskerville, Libre Baskerville still gives an impression of a font family from the storybook, which perfectly aligns with the semantics of our app, while it is fully adapted for the use on a screen. Nunito is a legible sans serif font with rounded shapes that works well for the body text. Nunito and Libre Baskerville is a perfect pairing with just enough contrast while typefaces still work in unison.

Conclusion

Sova was a passion project for me because I have strong empathy for parents who are trying to help their children to learn their heritage language. A platform for exchanging children's’ paper books in Russian is a much-needed product in the Russian community in North Carolina.

While working on the app I reached out to North Carolinian Russian-speaking parents and enjoyed working with them. I was amazed by their readiness to participate in user research, preference tests, and usability tests. Involving users at any stage of the digital product design is priceless.

Through numerous design iterations, user research, and usability tests I created a usable UI that is easy to navigate. Sova accomplishes its goal to give parents of bilingual children a convenient way to exchange paper books in Russian.
